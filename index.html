Inter-PIE developments:

Laryngeals colouring:

h₁ - neutral; h₂ - a-colouring; h₃ - o-colouring

Proto-Balto-Slavic:

Loss of Laryngeals:

*HV > *V; *VH > *V̄́ʔ

RUKI law (partly):

*{r,k}s > *{r,k}š;
*{i,u}s > *{i,u}s, sometimes *{i,u}š

Winter’s law (P̬ - voiced unaspirated stop):

*VP̬ > *V́P̬

Short o becomes short a (Winter’s law happened before)

*o > *a

Loss of labialisation and aspiration:

*Cʰ > *C; *Cʷ > *C (only voiced stops had aspiration and only velars had labialisation)

Satemisation:

*ḱ > *ś; *ǵ > *ź
sometimes : *ḱ > *k; *ǵ > *g
compare existing pairs: šleivas - kleivas (crooked); gnybti - žnybti (to pinch)

Syllabic consonants to sibilant-diphthongs:

*R̥ > *iR, sometimes *uR

Some other changes:

*m# > *n#
*V̄n# > *Vn#
*un > *ū́n
*t{t,d} > *st
*ew > *aw

Shit tons of accentuation changes I won’t cover

Proto-Baltic:

*Ci̭Vᶦᵉ > *CVᶦᵉ (Vᶦᵉ - front vowels)

Proto-Eastern-Baltic:

*ō > *uo (Latvians write <o> but they say /uo/)
*ei > *ie (not always, more prominent in Latvian)
*dl > *gl; *tl > *kl

Old-Lithuanian:

*VnP > VnP (P - plosive)
*Vn > V̨ (If plosive didn’t follow)
*{C,t,d}i̭Vᵃᵘᵒ > {C,č,dž}ʲVᵃᵘᵒ Vᵃᵘᵒ - back vowel

Endzelīns’ Law:

circumflex and acute inversion (but that's rather phonetic and not phonemic change IIRC, I'm not sure)

Fortunatov–de Saussure's law:

stress moves from stressed non-acute to the next syllable if the following syllable is acute.

Leskien’s Law (for word final syllable):

If a word isn’t monosyllabic
*V́ > V (includes nasal vowels too)
*íe > i
*úo > u
If a word is monosyllabic:
*V́ > Ṽ includes stuff like *íe > iẽ; *úo > uõ

Final touches:

*ē > ė
*ā > ō (in text represented by o)

*V̨ > V̄

*Cʲa( ̄ ) > Ce( ̄ )


Stressed *àσ, *èσ > stressed ãσ, ẽσ (mostly in the penultimate position, never in the final position)

Loss of intonation in unstressed syllables

Some notes:

V̀ - short
Ṽ - circumflex - long (or 2nd part in case of diphthong)
V́ - acute - long (or 1st part in case of diphthong)

In Proto-Lithuanian (and thus earlier) the stress wasn’t perhaps bounded to intonation


It’s hard to say when glide ṷ (w) became /v/ and /ṷ/, maybe in Proto-Baltic, maybe earlier, maybe later.
To understand what I mean compare:
kariauti (infinitive) vs kariavo (past 3rd person)
If we wrote it using w or ṷ, it would make total sense:
kariawti vs kariawo (so the stem is initially kariaw-).

Assimilating (de)voicing was possible already existing in Proto-Baltic
The same applies to final devoicing.
*ś > š change period is uknown
The same can be said about word final *-uš > -us

Current Lithuanian:

unstressed and not final ą, ę > a, e (dubious)
